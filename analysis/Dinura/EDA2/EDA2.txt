POVERTY-MIGRATION LINK AND LAGS

The longitudinal data for Sri Lanka (1994–2025) confirms a complex, non-linear relationship 
between the poverty headcount ratio(accrding to world bank metric of income under US$3.65 per day)
and annual emigration volumes. During the initial period, severe low income levels compared to overseas countries 
(reaching world bank poverty metric as high as 65.0%) correlated with growing but 
lower base volumes of emigration. This aligns with the survival migration, where 
extreme low incomes can actually act as a barrier to migration due to the high 
upfront costs of legal recruitment and placement.
As the income level compared to internation benchmark declined significantly 
to 18.6% by 2017, emigration did not decrease as one might expect. Instead, 
it surged and fluctuated at higher levels. This results in a classic migration hump
as household incomes rise slightly above the low income benchmark line, families 
gain the financial liquidity needed to facilitate the costs of international labor migration.
The Pearson correlation coefficient (r) for the dataset is -0.359 but While this indicates 
a weak-to-moderate negative relationship overall, the visual timeline suggests a distinct lag. 
The sharp reduction in poverty throughout the mid-2010s preceded the peak and subsequent plateauing 
of official emigration volumes.As the conclusion, While poverty remains a primary push factor, its reduction does not immediately 
lower emigration volumes. Rather, institutional factors and social networks 
sustain high migration rates even as income levelbenchmarks are surpassed.
